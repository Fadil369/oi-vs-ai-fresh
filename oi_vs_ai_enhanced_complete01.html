Training vs Inference: Two Phases of AI

AI development involves distinct phases with different computational requirements:

  1. Training Phase:
    • Massive datasets processed over weeks or months
    • Billions of parameters adjusted through backpropagation
    • Requires enormous computational resources
  2. Inference Phase:
    • Using trained models to generate responses
    • Must be fast, scalable, and efficient
    • Significantly less computational demand

Case Study: Training GPT-4

Scale: Estimated 1.7 trillion parameters trained on hundreds of billions of tokens

Resources: Thousands of high-end GPUs running for months

Cost: Estimated $100+ million in compute costs alone

Energy: Equivalent to several years of household electricity consumption

Chapter Summary

Understanding AI's digital infrastructure reveals both its remarkable capabilities and fundamental limitations. While AI achieves unprecedented scale and speed through silicon and software, it comes at enormous computational and environmental costs. The abstraction that enables AI's power also disconnects it from the embodied, efficient intelligence that biological systems have evolved over millions of years. This contrast highlights why human oversight and hybrid approaches may be essential for sustainable and beneficial AI development.

Complete Glossary of Terms

üìö
Intelligence Lexicon
Essential Terms for the AI Age

Core Intelligence Concepts

Original Intelligence (OI)
The natural, biological intelligence that humans are born with, shaped by millions of years of evolution. Characterized by consciousness, emotion, creativity, and moral reasoning.
Example: A doctor's intuitive sense that something is wrong with a patient despite normal test results.
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as learning, reasoning, and pattern recognition.
Example: Large language models like GPT that can generate human-like text responses.
Consciousness
The state of being aware of one's thoughts, feelings, and surroundings. A fundamental aspect of OI that current AI systems lack.
Example: The subjective experience of seeing the color red or feeling pain.
Agency
The capacity to act independently and make choices based on one's own will and intentions.
Example: A human choosing to help someone despite no external reward or requirement.
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to reorganize and form new neural connections throughout life, enabling learning and adaptation.
Example: Recovery of language abilities after a stroke through brain rewiring.

AI Technical Terms

Neural Network
A computing system inspired by biological neural networks, consisting of interconnected nodes that process information.
Example: The architecture underlying modern AI systems like ChatGPT.
Machine Learning
A subset of AI that enables computers to learn and improve from experience without being explicitly programmed for every task.
Example: A system that learns to recognize cats by analyzing thousands of cat photos.
Large Language Model (LLM)
An AI system trained on vast amounts of text data to understand and generate human-like language.
Example: GPT-4, Claude, or Gemini that can engage in conversations and complete text-based tasks.
Token
The basic unit of text processing in AI language models, typically representing words, parts of words, or punctuation.
Example: The sentence "Hello world!" might be tokenized as ["Hello", " world", "!"].
Training Data
The dataset used to teach an AI system, consisting of examples from which the system learns patterns and behaviors.
Example: Books, articles, and websites used to train language models.

Cognitive Concepts

Embodied Intelligence
Intelligence that is grounded in physical experience and sensory interaction with the world.
Example: Learning to ride a bicycle through physical practice and muscle memory.
Intuition
The ability to understand or know something immediately, without conscious reasoning or analysis.
Example: A mother sensing her child is upset before any obvious signs appear.
Context Window
The amount of recent conversation or text that an AI system can consider when generating responses.
Example: A model with a 32K token context window can "remember" about 24,000 words of recent conversation.
Emergent Behavior
Complex behaviors or capabilities that arise from simpler interactions, not explicitly programmed.
Example: Human consciousness emerging from billions of simple neural interactions.

Future Intelligence Concepts

Hybrid Intelligence
The combination of human and artificial intelligence working together to achieve better outcomes than either could alone.
Example: AI assisting doctors with diagnosis while humans make final treatment decisions.
Augmented Intelligence
AI designed to enhance human capabilities rather than replace them.
Example: AI tools that help writers edit their work while preserving their unique voice.
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
Hypothetical AI that matches or exceeds human cognitive abilities across all domains.
Example: An AI system that could perform any intellectual task a human can do.
Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)
Direct communication pathways between the brain and external devices.
Example: Neural implants that allow paralyzed patients to control computers with thought.
